Why the Tide Mechanic took three years to get right

47 discarded prototypes, two failed playtest sessions with professional reviewers, and one breakthrough moment in a friend's kitchen in Clifton. The full story of how Citadels of the Deep got its defining feature.

DO

David Osei

Lead Game Designer

March 18, 2026
12 min read
4,280 views

Timeline 3 years of iteration From random punishment to readable shared pressure.
Breakthrough Prototype v31 The kitchen playtest where the mechanic finally clicked.

Prototype v31 of the Tide Mechanic — the last version before we got it right. Photo: Elena Voss

The Tide Mechanic is the thing people talk about after playing Citadels of the Deep. Not the faction asymmetry, not the modular board, not the treaty system — the tides. Specifically, the moment the water rises and floods three of your carefully built fortifications, and you realise you should have seen it coming.

What they don't know is that version one of that mechanic was just a die roll. You rolled at the end of each round. Low numbers meant flooding. That's it. It was terrible.

The problem with randomness at the wrong moment

The original Tide was a punishment system. You built, you rolled, sometimes you lost. There was no meaningful decision point — just a tax on success that felt arbitrary and, frankly, unfair. Playtesters hated it uniformly.

But the core instinct was right. I wanted games to have a geological inevitability — the sense that forces larger than any player were at work, indifferent to anyone's plans. I just needed players to be able to read, and respond to, those forces.

"The best mechanics feel like physics. You don't fight them — you work within them. The Tide needed to feel like weather, not like a random penalty."

The second version gave each tide phase a visible countdown. You could see the water coming. That was better — but it created the opposite problem. Players optimised completely around the countdown, and the board became a game of pure accounting. Nobody was building fortresses because they wanted to. They were building them in whichever square the countdown said was safe.

Designer note: Versions 3 through 19 are documented in the TabletopForge design archive. If you ever back the Warlord tier, you'll get access to the full prototype library — including the embarrassing early builds.

The hidden information breakthrough

Prototype v20 introduced hidden tide cards. Each player secretly held a card showing which zone would flood next. You knew your card — but not anyone else's. This created real bluffing, real negotiation, and for the first time, the tides became a social lever rather than a random one.

But it broke two-player. With only two people, hidden information collapsed into pure deduction — you knew what you held, so you could always infer what your opponent held. The mechanic worked for four and five players but fell apart at the table sizes we needed most.

The kitchen playtest

It was Elena's kitchen. January 2025. We'd been testing v31 for three hours and something was almost working — but not quite. The tide was now driven by an evolving pressure track shared by all players, with each player contributing tokens that shifted the trajectory of flooding in different directions.

Halfway through the third act, Elena built in a zone she'd been avoiding for two hours. I asked why she'd changed her mind. She said: "Because you just moved that token. It changed where I thought the water would go."

That was it. That was the mechanic. Not hidden information, not a countdown, not a random roll — a shared, evolving pressure system where every player's action influenced the tide, and where reading other players' token placements was as important as reading the board itself.

What we shipped

The final Tide Mechanic uses a six-zone pressure track with four token types. Each round, players allocate tide tokens as part of their normal action economy — they're not a separate phase, not a tax, just part of what you do on your turn. The accumulation of tokens shifts the pressure, and the flood targets the highest-pressure zone at the end of each Act.

The elegant part — the part I'm most proud of — is that you're always doing two things with every token: managing your immediate position, and signalling something to every other player about where you want the water to go. Whether that signal is honest or not is your choice.

If you want to see the mechanic in action before your copy arrives, the print-and-play files are live in the Designer Studio. Every designer — including Scout tier — has access right now.
